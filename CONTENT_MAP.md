Body
- H2: "The Author and the Project"
- Paragraph 1:
  "There is a particular clarity that comes from being foreign, from navigating a city whose signs you cannot read, a conversation whose subtext escapes you, a ceremony whose meaning you must ask someone to explain. This project was born in that discomfort, and in the conviction that such discomfort is worth documenting."
- Paragraph 2:
  "Stories From Abroad is a personal dispatch, part literary journal, part field notebook. It exists in the tradition of writers who believed that observation, if patient enough, becomes a form of understanding. It is not journalism, exactly. It is not memoir, quite. It is something in between: witnessed and imagined, reported and felt."
- Paragraph 3:
  "The photography is shot on film where possible. The essays take as long as they need to. Each section has its own logic, but they share a common conviction: that the world is worth paying attention to."
- Paragraph 4:
  "If you have found your way here, you are warmly invited to stay. Subscribe to receive dispatches when new work is published. No algorithms, no advertising. Just writing, when it is ready."

Post 1
- Location: "Tbilisi, Georgia"
- Date: "Mar 8, 2026"

- Title: "The Wine is Free and the City Hums"
- Preview:
  "Nobody warned me that Tbilisi would smell like woodsmoke and roses all at once. I arrived at midnight to find the old town still breathing — cafes spilling onto cobblestones, someone’s grandmother leaning from a carved wooden balcony three floors up."
- Full text:
  "Nobody warned me that Tbilisi would smell like woodsmoke and roses all at once. I arrived at midnight to find the old town still breathing — cafes spilling onto cobblestones, someone’s grandmother leaning from a carved wooden balcony three floors up, a dog that seemed to own the street entirely.

  Georgian wine is served like water here. Not with ceremony. Just poured. And the food — I have been eating khinkali every single day. I have been eating them wrong every single day. Nobody has stopped me.

  The thing about Georgia nobody tells you: it is impossibly, stubbornly itself. Hundreds of years of being invaded and it still has its own alphabet, its own wine, its own polyphonic singing that makes you feel something in your chest you do not have a word for.

  I am staying in a guesthouse run by a woman named Nino who insists on feeding me breakfast even when I am trying to leave early. I am never trying that hard."

Post 2
- Location: "Marrakech, Morocco"
- Date: "Feb 21, 2026"
- Time: "3:17 PM"
- Title: "The Man Who Sold Silence"
- Preview:
  "He sat at the edge of the Djemaa el-Fna every evening with nothing in front of him. No snake, no spice, no carpet. Just a small sign: 10 dirhams for five minutes of quiet."
- Full text:
  "He sat at the edge of the Djemaa el-Fna every evening with nothing in front of him. No snake, no spice, no carpet. Just a small sign that read: 10 dirhams for five minutes of quiet.

  I paid him on a Tuesday.

  He gestured to the stool beside him, and I sat, and he folded his hands, and we watched the square together without speaking. The drums were still there. None of it went away. But he looked at it with such complete calm that the chaos became, briefly, something you could hold.

  After five minutes he raised a hand. That was it. I thanked him in broken Darija and he nodded once, like he already knew.

  I have thought about him a lot since then. What he was actually selling. Whether it was silence, or just the permission to stop."

Post 3
- Location: "Somewhere over the Black Sea"
- Date: "Feb 14, 2026"
- Time: "7:03 AM"
- Title: "On Being a Stranger"
- Preview:
  "There is a particular freedom in being somewhere no one has any expectations of you. You are just a shape moving through a city that has its own complete life without you."
- Full text:
  "There is a particular freedom in being somewhere no one has any expectations of you. You are just a shape moving through a city that has its own complete life without you. It does not need you. The city is fine.

  At home, I am a collection of things people know about me. Here I am just someone who ordered the wrong thing at dinner and laughed about it and walked home in the rain.

  I used to think travel was about seeing things. I think it is more about temporarily shedding the story you have built around yourself. Usually something simpler. Something more animal. A person who needs food and sleep and occasionally to watch the sea.

  What I keep wondering: why does it take this — thousands of miles and a language I cannot speak — to feel this uncomplicated?"

Post 4
- Location: "Istanbul, Turkey"
- Date: "Jan 30, 2026"
- Time: "6:22 PM"
- Title: "Straddling Two Continents for a Simit"
- Preview:
  "I walked across the Galata Bridge just to buy a simit from the Asian side. This is the kind of decision that travel turns you into — a person who makes small unnecessary pilgrimages because the geography feels meaningful."
- Full text:
  "I walked across the Galata Bridge just to buy a simit from the Asian side. This is the kind of decision that travel turns you into: a person who makes small unnecessary pilgrimages because the geography of it feels meaningful.

  Istanbul is overwhelming in the best possible way. It is a city that has absorbed empires and spat them out and kept the good parts of each. Byzantine mosaics under Ottoman domes. Greek ruins next to Roman aqueducts.

  I drank cay in a tea garden overlooking the Bosphorus and watched the tankers go past. Enormous, lumbering, completely out of place next to the little fishing boats. That image felt like the whole city somehow.

  Ferries here are not transport. They are philosophy."
