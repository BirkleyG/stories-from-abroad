About

The Author
& the Project

Writing from the spaces between belonging.

There is a particular clarity that comes from being foreign — from navigating a city whose signs you cannot read, a conversation whose subtext escapes you, a ceremony whose meaning you must ask someone to explain. This project was born in that discomfort, and in the conviction that such discomfort is worth documenting.

Stories From Abroad is a personal dispatch — part literary journal, part field notebook. It exists in the tradition of writers who believed that observation, if patient enough, becomes a form of understanding. It is not journalism, exactly. It is not memoir, quite. It is something in between: witnessed and imagined, reported and felt.

The photography is shot on film where possible. The essays take as long as they need to. The fiction draws on real places and invented people. Each section has its own logic, but they share a common conviction: that the world is worth paying attention to.

❧

Academically, the work is informed by studies in international relations and political economy — a framework that colours how power and place are understood in these pages, even when the subject is something as small as a shared meal or a missed train.

If you've found your way here, you're warmly invited to stay. Subscribe to receive dispatches when new work is published — no algorithms, no advertising. Just writing, when it's ready.

Essay 14 November 2025

On Belonging Nowhere in Particular

What movement teaches us about home

12 min read
❧

There is a photograph on my phone — taken three years ago, somewhere in the Balkans — of a kitchen window with a cracked sill and a potted geranium that had given up flowering. I kept it not for aesthetic reasons but because something about it felt like the truth of a place: the ordinary persistence of things that haven't quite managed to be beautiful.

I have lived in eleven cities across four continents. I do not say this to impress — it is, in fact, increasingly difficult to explain without sounding either boastful or evasive — but because it is the condition from which this writing comes. A person who has lived in eleven cities is not an expert in any of them. But they have developed a sensitivity to the texture of unfamiliarity that has its own uses.

"To be at home everywhere is to be at home nowhere. But there are worse fates than that."

The Grammar of Elsewhere

Every new city requires a period of grammatical study — not the language, necessarily, but the underlying logic of how the place works. Where do people eat on a Tuesday? What time does quiet arrive? What is the unspoken rule about eye contact on public transport? These are the things no guidebook tells you, and learning them takes months.

The philosopher Simone Weil wrote about the need for roots — the hunger, she argued, is one of the most important and most overlooked of the human soul. I believe her. And yet I have found, over years of deliberate rootlessness, that roots need not be geographical. They can be carried.
